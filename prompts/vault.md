## Sourcing (finding businesses)

### **Build a target list from a vertical**
*When: You know the industry and geography, need a scored list of prospects.*
```
You are an acquisition analyst. Find [20] [B2B professional services] businesses in [Orange County, CA] meeting these criteria: [$1-5M revenue], [$300K-1M SDE], owner-operated, established [5+ years]. For each business: pull the listing or company details, score against the 13 AQ criteria (location, seller finance willingness, margin, staff, own property, training, broker, long listing, history, FF&E, multiple, leased space, inventory), flag any reds. Deliver as a markdown table: Business Name | Revenue | SDE | AQ Score Summary | Reds | Contact. Rank by attractiveness.
```

### **Monitor on-market listings**
*When: You want to track new listings matching your buy box in real time.*
```
Search BizBuySell, LoopNet, and business broker sites for new listings in the past [7 days] matching: [industry], [geography], [revenue range], [asking price range]. For each listing: extract business name, asking price, revenue, cash flow, broker, time listed, and link. Deliver as a table. Flag any that match my buy box: [paste buy-box criteria].
```

### **Research a vertical for acquisition opportunities**
*When: You are exploring a new industry and need the landscape mapped.*
```
You are a market researcher. Research the [home services HVAC] industry in [Southern California]. Provide: (1) market size and growth trends, (2) typical business financials (revenue, margins, multiples), (3) competitive landscape (fragmented vs consolidated), (4) acquisition opportunities (baby-boomer exits, distressed assets, roll-up potential), (5) red flags specific to this vertical. Cite sources.
```

### **Find off-market acquisition targets**
*When: You want to source businesses not actively listed.*
```
You are a deal sourcing strategist. Help me identify [15-20] off-market [B2B software/SaaS] companies in [geographic area] that fit my criteria: [revenue/profit/owner profile]. Provide: company names, estimated revenue, owner demographics (age, time in business), likely exit motivation (retirement, burnout, health), and contact approach (LinkedIn, letter, referral). Deliver as a table.
```

## Qualification (screening deals)

### **Score a target against AQ criteria**
*When: You have a CIM or listing and need a fast go/no-go.*
```
You are an acquisition analyst. I am uploading a [CIM/listing] for [business name]. Score it against the 13 AQ criteria: Location, Seller Finance, Broker, Long Listing, Margin, History, FF&E, Multiple, Leased Space, Own Property, Staff, Inventory, Training. Use green (favorable), yellow (unknown/neutral), red (unfavorable). Deliver as a table with 13 rows: Criterion | Score | Notes. Follow with a go/no-go recommendation and reasoning.
```

### **Extract red flags from a CIM**
*When: You need to spot dealbreakers fast.*
```
You are a due diligence analyst. Review this CIM for red flags in these categories: (1) customer concentration (any customer >30% of revenue), (2) owner dependence (business cannot run without owner), (3) declining revenue or profit trend, (4) missing or incomplete financials, (5) legal/compliance issues, (6) single-product or single-channel risk, (7) lease or real estate problems. List every red flag found with severity (dealbreaker / caution / monitor). If no reds, say so.
```

### **Run the 70% Rule first-call screen**
*When: You just got off a first call and need to decide whether to continue.*
```
You are an acquisition advisor. I just spoke with the owner of [business name]. Here are the facts: [summarize: revenue, profit, owner motivation, asking price, time listed, willingness to finance, team situation, financials availability]. Apply the 70% Rule: kill this deal fast if it has dealbreaker reds. Check: customer concentration, owner dependence, declining numbers, unrealistic price, no financials, legal issues. Recommend: KILL (70% Rule apply, move on) or PURSUE (green enough to continue). Explain reasoning.
```

### **Build a seller qualification scorecard**
*When: You are tracking multiple conversations and need a system.*
```
Create a seller qualification scorecard template for tracking [10] active conversations. Columns: Seller Name | Business | First Contact Date | AQ Score Summary | Red Flags | Financials Received (Y/N) | Next Step | Priority (High/Med/Low). Pre-fill with placeholders I can update as I gather data.
```

## Outreach (contacting owners)

### **Draft a cold outreach email (investor angle)**
*When: You are reaching out to an owner with no prior relationship.*
```
You are a deal sourcing copywriter. Draft a cold email to [owner name] of [business name] in [industry]. Angle: I am a private investor looking to acquire a business in [industry/geography]. Tone: professional, respectful, brief (under 150 words). Include: (1) who I am, (2) what I am looking for, (3) why their business caught my attention (be specific: [name a detail from their website/listing]), (4) soft call-to-action (open to a brief call to explore?). No aggressive language. End with my contact info: [name], [phone], [email].
```

### **Draft a cold outreach email (strategic buyer angle)**
*When: You own a related business and can frame it as partnership/referral.*
```
You are a deal sourcing copywriter. Draft a cold email to [owner name] of [business name]. Angle: I own a [related business type] in [location] and occasionally have customers looking for [their services/products]. I would love to explore referring business to each other and see if there is a fit for a deeper partnership or acquisition. Tone: collaborative, low-pressure. Length: under 150 words. End with: suggest a call next week, include my contact info.
```

Your company came up in my research. Requesting connection to explore possibilities. Tone: professional, direct.

### **Write a direct mail letter (off-market outreach)**

## Discovery (deep qualification)

### **Generate the 50-question seller questionnaire**
*When: You are preparing for a deep-dive discovery call.*
```
You are an acquisition analyst. Generate the EPIC 50-question seller questionnaire in 6 sections: (1) Story & Motivation (why selling, timeline, transition plan), (2) Operations (daily workflow, owner role, team structure, systems), (3) Revenue & Customers (top customers, concentration, churn, contracts, acquisition channels), (4) Profitability (margins, add-backs, owner salary, discretionary expenses), (5) Marketing & Sales (lead gen, conversion, customer lifetime value), (6) Systems & Assets (software, IP, equipment, real estate, inventory). Deliver as a numbered list I can use on a call.
```

### **Analyze discovery call notes**
*When: You just finished a discovery call and have raw notes.*
```
You are an acquisition analyst. I just completed a discovery call with [owner name] of [business name]. Here are my raw notes: [paste notes]. Extract: (1) owner motivation (why selling, urgency), (2) financials discussed (revenue, SDE, add-backs), (3) team and operations (who does what, can it run without owner), (4) customer base (top 3 customers, concentration %), (5) red flags or concerns, (6) green flags (seller financing interest, transition willingness, real estate owned), (7) next steps (what info to request). Deliver as structured sections.
```

### **Request financials (email template)**

```
Draft an email to [seller/broker] requesting financials for [business name]. Tone: professional, standard process. Frame as: "standard due diligence, will sign NDA if not already in place." Keep under 100 words.
```

## Valuation (pricing the deal)

### **Build a 3-comp valuation model**
*When: You need to price a business using comparable sales.*
```
You are a valuation analyst. Build a 3-comp valuation model for [business name]: [$2.1M revenue], [$620K SDE], [B2B consulting], [Orange County CA]. Search for comparable sales (same industry, similar size, last 24 months). For each comp: extract business name, revenue, SDE, sale price, multiple. Normalize all comps to SDE multiples. Deliver as a markdown table: Comp | Revenue | SDE | Sale Price | Multiple | Source. Follow with: fair-value range for the target, recommended offer range, reasoning for adjustments.
```

### **Calculate realistic valuation multiples**
*When: You need to ground expectations on what multiples apply.*
```
You are a valuation expert. Explain the valuation multiple landscape for [industry] businesses at [$X-Y revenue] and [$A-B SDE]. Provide: (1) owner-operated multiple range (typically 2.4x SDE), (2) professionally managed multiple range (typically 4.0x EBITDA), (3) factors that push multiples higher (recurring revenue, growth, IP, real estate), (4) factors that push multiples lower (customer concentration, owner dependence, declining trends). Cite industry norms.
```

### **Identify add-backs and normalize SDE**
*When: The seller's financials include personal expenses you need to adjust.*
```
You are a financial analyst. Review this P&L for [business name]. Identify legitimate add-backs (owner salary above replacement cost, personal expenses run through the business, one-time costs, discretionary spending) and calculate normalized SDE. Critical: subtract the cost to replace the owner's role BEFORE trusting the SDE (if owner works 60 hours/week doing sales + ops, hiring a replacement costs $X). Deliver: (1) list of add-backs with amounts, (2) normalized SDE, (3) adjusted valuation multiple if SDE changes materially.
```

### **Value a traffic/marketing asset (ZDOT method)**
*When: You are a Stage-3 searcher evaluating a $0-traffic asset (email list, social account, group, SEO property, show/podcast, small digital property) rather than an operating business.*
```
You are an acquisition analyst using the EPIC ZDOT traffic-asset method. I am evaluating this asset: [describe: type (list/social/group/SEO/show/IP), audience size, engagement, niche]. My current customer economics: [target CAC $X], [AOV $Y], [conversion assumptions if known]. Model the asset as a mini-business: Monthly Buyers = Traffic x Conversion Rate; Rev/Month = Buyers x AOV; Rev/Year = x12; Net Profit = Rev/Year - 12-month ops cost; ZDOT CAC = ops cost / buyers. Compare ZDOT CAC to my current CAC — negative delta means the asset acquires customers cheaper than I do today, and the annual savings is the value of owning it. Use ONLY numbers I provide or that we verify; label every assumption. Deliver: the per-asset value model, suggested max offer (lower of ask vs. value), and what data I still need from the seller.
```

### **Run the tax-return test**
*When: You need to verify the P&L matches the tax returns.*
```
You are a forensic accountant. I am uploading [3 years of P&L] and [3 years of tax returns (Schedule C or corporate returns)] for [business name]. Compare: does the reported revenue and profit on the P&L match the tax returns? Flag any discrepancies (>5% variance). If they do not match, flag as a red (either the P&L is inflated or the owner is underreporting to the IRS — both are problems). Deliver: year-by-year comparison table + red flag summary.
```

## Negotiation (structuring the offer)

### **Build a walk-away / realistic / dream range**
*When: You are preparing for negotiation and need your ranges locked before the room.*
```
You are a negotiation strategist. I am preparing to negotiate for [business name]: [$2.1M asking price], [$620K SDE]. Help me define: (1) WALK-AWAY (the price above which I walk — typically fair value or slightly above), (2) REALISTIC (the price I expect to land at — typically 10-20% below asking if the business is fairly priced), (3) DREAM (the best possible outcome — seller financing, earnout, equity rollover, price reduction). Ground in: SDE multiples (2.4x owner-op, 4.0x managed), comps, and red flags. Deliver as a table: Range | Price | Terms | Reasoning.
```

### **Draft a negotiation strategy (price or terms, not both)**
*When: You are entering negotiation and need a game plan.*
```
You are a negotiation advisor. I am negotiating for [business name] at [$X asking price]. Seller motivation: [retirement, burned out, health, moving]. Roland Frasier's rule: "You can have your price OR your terms, not both." Build a strategy: (1) if I pay asking price, what terms do I demand (seller financing %, earnout %, consulting offset, equity rollover), (2) if I demand standard terms (20% down, 80% seller note), what price reduction do I push for. Frame both paths. Deliver as two scenarios with reasoning.
```

### **Apply the 24-hour rule**
*When: You are in active negotiation and need discipline.*
```
You are a negotiation coach. Explain the 24-hour rule: never accept or reject an offer in the room. Always take 24 hours to think, run numbers, and consult advisors. Draft a response template I can use when a seller makes an offer: "Thank you for the offer. I need 24 hours to review the numbers and discuss with my team. I will get back to you by [date/time]." Keep it professional and non-committal.
```

### **Counter an aggressive offer**
*When: The seller's terms are unrealistic and you need to reset expectations.*
```
You are a negotiation strategist. The seller of [business name] is asking [$X], all cash, no financing, no earnout. My analysis shows fair value is [$Y] (based on [SDE multiple / comps]). Draft a professional counter-offer email: (1) acknowledge their position, (2) explain my valuation reasoning (cite comps, multiples, reds), (3) propose structure: [$Z down], [$A seller note over 5 years], [$B earnout tied to revenue retention], (4) frame as win-win (certainty of close, tax benefits of installment sale, legacy protected). Tone: firm but respectful. Under 250 words.
```

### **Frame seller motivation (certainty + legacy > +$50K)**
*When: You need to shift the seller's focus from price to deal certainty.*
```
You are a deal psychologist. The seller of [business name] is fixated on getting [$X asking price], which is [20%] above fair value. Help me reframe the conversation around: (1) certainty of close (I am a serious buyer, have financing lined up, can close in [timeframe]), (2) legacy (I will protect the business, keep the team, honor their reputation), (3) tax benefits (installment sale spreads tax liability vs. lump-sum hit). Draft talking points I can use to shift focus from +$50K in price to these benefits. Deliver as bullet points.
```

## Financing (structuring the deal)

### **Build a financing STACK (seller financing + earnout + consulting offset)**
*When: You need to structure a $0-cash-at-closing deal.*
```
You are a creative deal structuring advisor. I am buying [business name] for [$1.8M]. Owner is [68, retiring, open to seller financing]. Build a financing STACK to get to $0 cash at closing using: (1) seller financing (5-year promissory note, [%] interest, [monthly/quarterly] payments), (2) earnout (tied to [revenue retention / profit growth], [% of purchase price], [timeframe]), (3) consulting offset (owner stays on as advisor for [X months], $[Y]/month fee applied to purchase price). Show the math: how much each strategy covers, what the seller receives at closing vs. over time. Deliver as a table: Strategy | Amount | Terms | Timing. Follow with seller narrative (why this works for them).
```

### **Select strategies from the 13 EPIC funding families**
*When: You need creative options beyond standard seller financing.*
```
You are a deal financing strategist. I am structuring a deal for [business name] at [$X purchase price]. Review the 13 EPIC funding families: (A) Earn-In / Sweat Equity, (B) Seller-Financed / Owner-Carry, (C) Seller Consulting / IP Offsets, (D) Asset Carveouts & Consignment, (E) Stock Buy-Back & Merger, (F) 3rd-Party Equity & Co-Investors, (G) Debt Assumption & Restructuring, (H) Lender / Institutional Financing, (I) Cashflow-Assignment Down Payments, (J) Pre-Sale / Promotion Funding, (K) Credit / Round-Robin / Short-Term Bridges, (L) Supplier / Vendor / AR / Inventory Financing, (M) Arbitrage / Flip / Advanced. Recommend 3-5 strategies that fit this deal based on: [business details: assets, inventory, customer list, owner willingness, real estate, etc.]. Deliver as a list with brief explanations.
```

### **Run a 4DCM (4 Day Cash Machine) scenario**
*When: You want to test pre-selling to fund the down payment.*
```
You are a pre-sale strategist. I am considering a 4DCM play for [business name]: run a promotion to the company's customer list BEFORE closing, use the revenue to fund the down payment. The business has [X customers], average order value [$Y], email list of [Z]. Model a conservative scenario: (1) promotion offer (discount, urgency, new product bundle), (2) expected conversion rate ([1-3%]), (3) revenue generated, (4) how much of the down payment this covers. Assess risk: will this burn goodwill or damage the brand? Deliver as: scenario model + risk assessment.
```

### **Assume seller debt as part of the purchase price**
*When: The seller has existing debt you can take on to reduce cash needed.*
```
You are a deal structuring advisor. The seller of [business name] has [$X in existing debt] (equipment loans, SBA loan, etc.). Propose a structure where I assume this debt as part of the purchase price. Show the math: asking price [$Y], minus assumed debt [$X], equals cash/seller financing needed [$Z]. What are the risks? (loan terms, personal guaranty, lender consent to transfer). Deliver as: structure summary + risk checklist.
```

### **Evaluate SBA 7(a) loan for acquisition**
*When: You need institutional financing and SBA is an option.*
```
You are an SBA lending expert. I am buying [business name] for [$X]. Evaluate whether an SBA 7(a) loan fits: (1) eligibility (business type, size, use of proceeds), (2) down payment required (typically 10-20%), (3) terms (interest rate, amortization, guaranty), (4) seller financing compatibility (SBA allows seller note as part of capital structure). Recommend: pursue SBA loan (and how much), combine with seller financing (and how), or skip it (and why). Deliver as: recommendation + structure.
```

## Diligence (verifying the deal)

### **Build a due diligence checklist**
*When: You are entering DD and need a comprehensive task list.*
```
You are a due diligence manager. Create a due diligence checklist for acquiring [business name] in [industry]. Categories: (1) Financial (P&L verification, tax return match, add-back validation, customer concentration, AR aging), (2) Legal (contracts, leases, licenses, litigation, IP ownership), (3) Operational (team org chart, key-person dependence, systems documentation, vendor agreements), (4) Customer (top 10 customers, churn rate, contract terms, revenue concentration), (5) Assets (equipment condition, inventory valuation, real estate title if applicable). Deliver as a checklist with checkboxes.
```

### **Verify customer concentration risk**
*When: You need to assess how dependent the business is on top customers.*
```
You are a risk analyst. I am uploading a customer revenue list for [business name]. Calculate: (1) revenue concentration (% from top customer, top 3, top 10), (2) churn risk (are top customers on contracts or at-will?), (3) mitigation plan (how to reduce concentration post-close). Flag as RED if any single customer >30% of revenue. Deliver as: concentration table + risk rating + mitigation recommendations.
```

### **Review contracts and leases**
*When: You need to confirm key agreements transfer cleanly.*
```
You are a contracts analyst. Review the uploaded contracts for [business name]: customer agreements, vendor agreements, lease, equipment financing. For each: (1) does it transfer to a new owner (assignable or needs consent?), (2) are there change-of-control clauses that could trigger termination?, (3) are terms favorable or problematic (locked in low pricing, auto-renewal, penalty clauses)? Flag any that require renegotiation or seller consent. Deliver as: contract-by-contract summary table.
```

### **Validate add-backs with documentation**
*When: The seller claims add-backs but you need proof.*
```
You are a forensic accountant. The seller of [business name] claims [$X in add-backs]: [list: owner salary, personal vehicle, travel, etc.]. I am uploading the P&L and supporting documentation (receipts, payroll records, credit card statements). For each add-back: verify it is legitimate (personal expense or above-market owner comp) and supported by documentation. Flag any that are questionable or unsupported. Deliver as: add-back validation table (Claimed | Verified | Documentation | Valid Y/N).
```

## Closing (finalizing the deal)

### **Draft a Letter of Intent (LOI)**
*When: You are ready to submit a formal offer.*
```
You are a deal attorney (informational only, not legal advice). Draft a Letter of Intent for acquiring [business name]. Include: (1) purchase price [$X], (2) structure (down payment [$Y], seller note [$Z over N years at R% interest], earnout [$A tied to metric]), (3) due diligence period ([30-60 days]), (4) contingencies (financials verified, no material adverse change, lease transfer), (5) exclusivity period ([30-60 days]), (6) timeline to close. Tone: professional, standard terms. Note: have a lawyer review before submitting.
```

### **Build a closing checklist**
*When: You are approaching close and need to track final tasks.*
```
You are a closing coordinator. Create a closing checklist for [business name]. Tasks: (1) finalize purchase agreement (attorney review), (2) secure financing (loan approval, seller note executed), (3) transfer licenses and permits, (4) execute assignment of contracts (customer agreements, vendor agreements, lease), (5) transfer assets (equipment, inventory, IP, domain, social accounts), (6) set up new legal entity (LLC, corp), (7) notify customers and vendors, (8) transition employees (offer letters, payroll setup), (9) obtain insurance (liability, property, E&O), (10) close escrow and fund. Deliver as a checklist with deadlines.
```

### **Plan a 90-day transition with the seller**
*When: The seller is staying on to train and you need a handoff plan.*
```
You are a transition manager. The seller of [business name] is staying on for [90 days] post-close to train and transition. Build a 90-day plan: (1) Week 1-2 (shadow seller, meet customers and team, document processes), (2) Week 3-6 (seller introduces me to customers, I take over day-to-day ops with seller available), (3) Week 7-12 (seller transitions to advisor role, available for questions but not daily operations). Deliver as a week-by-week task list.
```

### **Draft a customer transition announcement**
*When: You need to tell customers about the ownership change.*
```
You are a business communications writer. Draft an email to customers of [business name] announcing the ownership transition. Tone: positive, reassuring, brief (under 200 words). Include: (1) I am the new owner, (2) [seller name] is transitioning [staying on as advisor / retiring], (3) no changes to service, quality, or team (if true), (4) I am excited to serve them and grow the business, (5) my contact info. Keep it warm and professional.
```

## Modernization (post-acquisition growth)

### **Audit operations for automation opportunities**
*When: You own the business and want to find efficiency gains.*
```
You are an operations consultant. I just acquired [business name] in [industry]. The business is [owner-operated, manual processes, no CRM, paper invoices, etc.]. Audit for automation opportunities: (1) lead generation and CRM (move to [HubSpot, Salesforce, etc.]), (2) invoicing and payments (automate AR with [Stripe, QuickBooks, etc.]), (3) customer communication (email sequences, chatbots), (4) reporting (automate financial dashboards), (5) team communication (Slack, project management tools). For each: recommend a tool, estimate time saved, estimate cost. Deliver as a table: Process | Current State | Automation Tool | Time Saved | Cost.
```

### **Build a 12-month growth plan**
*When: You want to scale the business post-acquisition.*
```
You are a growth strategist. I own [business name]: [$X revenue], [$Y SDE]. Build a 12-month growth plan focused on: (1) customer acquisition (new channels, paid ads, partnerships, referrals), (2) customer retention (reduce churn, upsell existing customers), (3) pricing optimization (test price increases, package changes), (4) operational efficiency (automate, reduce waste, improve margins). For each initiative: define the goal, the tactic, the timeline, and the expected revenue/profit impact. Deliver as a roadmap with quarterly milestones.
```

### **Identify cross-sell opportunities in the portfolio**
*When: You own multiple businesses and want to unlock synergies.*
```
You are a portfolio strategist. I own [N businesses]: [list names, industries, customer bases]. Identify cross-sell opportunities: (1) which customer bases overlap or are adjacent?, (2) which products/services can be bundled?, (3) which businesses can refer to each other?. For each opportunity: describe the tactic, estimate revenue lift, estimate effort required. Deliver as a table: Business A | Business B | Cross-Sell Tactic | Revenue Lift | Effort.
```

### **Hire a general manager to remove yourself**
*When: You want to professionalize the business and step back.*
```
You are a hiring consultant. I own [business name] and want to hire a general manager to run day-to-day operations so I can step back. Define the role: (1) responsibilities (P&L ownership, team management, customer relationships, ops), (2) required experience (industry, company size, skills), (3) compensation structure (salary + bonus tied to profit growth, equity optional), (4) success metrics (revenue, profit, team retention, customer satisfaction). Deliver as: job description + comp structure + 90-day success plan.
```

- Replace `[bracketed placeholders]` with your specifics before running.
- Negotiation prompts ground in: walk-away/realistic/dream ranges, 24-hour rule, "price or terms not both."
- Financing prompts ground in: the 13 EPIC funding families, STACK multiple strategies to $0 cash.
- Qualification prompts ground in: the 13 AQ criteria (Location, Seller Finance, Broker, Long Listing, Margin, History, FF&E, Multiple, Leased Space, Own Property, Staff, Inventory, Training).
- Save prompts you use frequently as skills in Claude for one-command reuse.
