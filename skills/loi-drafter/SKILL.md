# LOI Drafter

## Overview

The Letter of Intent builder. This skill generates a complete, legally-structured LOI after you have completed valuation, set your walk-away/realistic/dream prices, and designed your financing stack (from deal-stacker). The LOI includes purchase price, payment terms, earnout provisions (if any), seller transition and training commitments, due diligence timeline, exclusivity period, and closing conditions. It follows Roland Frasier's principle: "You can have your price OR your terms, not both." If the seller insists on their asking price, you get favorable terms (long seller financing, earnouts, consulting offsets). If you hit your dream price (below asking), you offer more seller-friendly terms (shorter holdback, higher interest rate, faster payment).

## When to Use

Run loi-drafter after you have completed negotiation prep: (1) valuation is done and you have walk-away/realistic/dream prices documented, (2) financing stack is designed (from deal-stacker) and you know your cash-at-closing target, (3) you understand the seller's priorities (certainty vs. speed vs. maximum dollars vs. legacy/employee retention), (4) you are ready to make a written offer. Do not draft an LOI before these steps are complete. The LOI is a binding commitment (morally, sometimes legally). Treat it seriously.

## Steps

1. **Apply the 24-hour rule.** Frasier's version is stated to the seller upfront: "I never make decisions on the spot — no matter how good this sounds, I always take 24 hours to think things through." It protects you from pressure tactics, FOMO manipulation, and seller urgency games; if a seller says "decide today or the deal is off," that is your cue to walk (they usually backtrack). So after the final negotiation conversation, do not send the LOI immediately. Sleep on it. Review your valuation, your financing stack, and your walk-away price. Make sure the deal still makes sense in the cold light of day. If you have any doubt, revise the terms or walk away. The 24-hour rule prevents deal fever and emotional commitments.

2. **Confirm the price-vs-terms trade.** Decide which path you are taking: (A) Seller gets their asking price, you get favorable terms (long seller note, earnout, consulting offset, minimal cash at closing), or (B) Seller accepts a lower price (your dream or realistic price), you offer more seller-friendly terms (higher interest rate, shorter balloon, more cash upfront). You cannot give both a low price and seller-unfriendly terms. The seller will reject it.

3. **Integrate the financing stack.** Pull the chosen stack from deal-stacker. Each component (seller note, earnout, consulting agreement, asset carveout, debt assumption, etc.) becomes a line item in Section 3 (Purchase Price and Payment Terms) of the LOI. Specify amounts, timing, interest rates (if any), security (secured vs. unsecured), and conditions.

4. **Draft the LOI.** Paste Prompt 1 below with your deal facts. Claude generates a complete LOI with standard sections: (1) Parties and Target, (2) Purchase Price, (3) Payment Terms, (4) Earnout (if applicable), (5) Seller Transition and Training, (6) Due Diligence Period, (7) Exclusivity, (8) Closing Conditions, (9) Confidentiality, (10) Non-Binding (except exclusivity and confidentiality). Review carefully.

5. **Refine and finalize.** Ask Claude to adjust any terms that do not match your negotiation outcome. Common refinements: extend the DD period, add a financing contingency, adjust the earnout formula, specify transition duration, clarify what happens if the seller does not deliver financials on time.

6. **Send to your attorney.** The LOI generated by Claude is a starting template, not a final legal document. Send it to your business attorney for review. They will add jurisdiction-specific clauses, tighten the language, and ensure it is enforceable. Do not send the LOI to the seller until your attorney approves it.

7. **Deliver the LOI.** Send the LOI to the seller with a cover email or call. Reiterate the key terms verbally to avoid surprises. Set a response deadline (typically 3 to 5 business days). If the seller accepts, move to due diligence. If they counter, evaluate the counter against your walk-away price and terms. If they reject outright, ask why and decide whether to revise or walk.

## The Prompts

**Prompt 1: Generate LOI from Deal Terms**

```
Draft a Letter of Intent for this acquisition:

Buyer: [my name or entity name]
Seller: [seller name]
Target business: [business name, entity type, state]
Industry: [specific industry/niche]

Price and terms:
- Purchase price: [total price]
- Payment structure: [insert the financing stack from deal-stacker, line by line. Example: "$500K cash at closing, $1M seller note at 5% interest over 5 years with $200K balloon at year 5, $300K earnout tied to year-2 SDE exceeding $400K, $50K/year consulting agreement for 3 years"]

Seller transition:
- Training period: [X weeks full-time, then X weeks part-time]
- Consulting period: [if part of the financing stack, specify duration]
- Non-compete: [geographic radius and duration, typically 50 miles / 3 years]

Due diligence:
- Duration: [30, 45, or 60 days]
- Key items to review: [financials, contracts, customer list, IP, legal/tax compliance, employee agreements]

Exclusivity:
- Duration: [30, 45, or 60 days matching DD period]

Closing:
- Target closing date: [X days after DD completion]
- Conditions: [financing secured, no material adverse change, key employees agree to stay, lease assignment approved]

Generate the LOI with standard legal structure. Mark the non-binding vs. binding sections clearly (only exclusivity and confidentiality are binding, rest is non-binding). Include a signature block for both parties.
```

**Prompt 2: Adjust Terms After Seller Counteroffer**

```
The seller countered my LOI with these changes:
- [List the seller's requested changes, e.g. "wants $100K more cash at closing", "wants earnout threshold lowered to $350K SDE", "wants 2-year non-compete instead of 3"]

My walk-away price is [price]. My walk-away terms are [terms]. Evaluate the counteroffer. Does it exceed my walk-away? If yes, recommend: accept, counter back, or walk. If I counter back, what should I propose to stay within my limits while addressing the seller's concerns?
```

**Prompt 3: Add a Financing Contingency**

```
I need to add a financing contingency to the LOI because I am pursuing SBA 7(a) financing and the loan is not yet approved. Draft the clause. It should state: If I cannot secure financing on acceptable terms within [X days], I can terminate the LOI without penalty and the earnest money (if any) is returned. Make sure this does not give me an indefinite out. The financing effort must be good-faith.
```

## The Loop

The LOI is the start of the formal deal process, not the end. After the seller signs, you enter the due diligence period (use dd-engine for this phase). During DD, you will likely discover new facts (customer concentration, undisclosed liabilities, declining trend). These facts may justify renegotiation. The LOI is non-binding except for exclusivity and confidentiality, so you can walk away or revise terms if DD reveals material issues. However, walking away after signing an LOI damages your reputation. Only sign LOIs for deals you genuinely intend to close. If you have doubt, do more discovery before drafting the LOI.

## Pitfalls

1. **Skipping the 24-hour rule.** Deal fever is real. After a long negotiation, you want to "get it done" and send the LOI immediately. This leads to regret. Giving yourself 24 hours to review the terms with fresh eyes prevents bad commitments. If the seller pressures you to send the LOI same-day, it is a red flag. A good seller respects your diligence.

2. **Giving the seller both their price and favorable terms.** If the seller demands their $2M asking price and you agree, you must get favorable terms in return (long seller financing, earnout, consulting offset, minimal cash upfront). If you give them $2M with $1.5M cash at closing and a short seller note, you overpaid and gave up all leverage. Apply the "price OR terms, not both" rule ruthlessly.

3. **Vague or missing payment terms.** "Seller financing of $500K" is not enough. You need: interest rate (or zero percent if negotiated), amortization schedule (interest-only with balloon, or straight amortization), security (secured by business assets or unsecured), default remedies, prepayment terms. Vague terms lead to disputes at closing. Be specific.

4. **Unrealistic due diligence timelines.** A 30-day DD period is standard for small businesses with clean books. If the business is complex (multiple locations, large customer base, regulatory issues), you need 45 to 60 days. Do not compress DD to please the seller. Rushed diligence misses red flags. Match the DD timeline to the deal complexity.

5. **Not involving an attorney before sending the LOI.** Claude generates a solid template, but it is not legal advice. State laws vary on LOI enforceability, earnout language, and closing conditions. Your attorney will catch jurisdiction-specific issues and tighten the language. Sending an LOI without legal review is amateur hour. It costs $500 to $1,500 for an attorney to review an LOI. Pay it.

## Output

You receive a complete Letter of Intent document formatted with standard sections, ready to send to your attorney for review. The LOI includes: parties, purchase price, payment structure (integrated from your financing stack), earnout formula (if applicable), seller transition commitments, due diligence timeline, exclusivity period, closing conditions, and signature blocks. The document clearly marks which sections are binding (only exclusivity and confidentiality) and which are non-binding (everything else). After attorney review and any final refinements, you have a ready-to-send LOI. Deliver it to the seller, set a response deadline, and prepare to enter due diligence if they accept.
