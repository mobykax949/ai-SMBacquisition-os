# Strategy Starter Pack — 20 Financing Strategies

Representative strategies from the 13 EPIC funding families. Use these to build financing STACKS (combine 3-5 strategies to reach $0 cash at closing).

*Note: This is the FREE starter kit. The paid community edition includes the full 216-strategy taxonomy.*

*These are financing concepts, not legal, tax, or lending advice. Several (debt assumption, credit bridges, pre-close promotions) carry real legal and tax consequences — run any structure past your attorney, CPA, and lender before you sign anything.*

---

## Family A: Earn-In / Sweat Equity

### **Instant Earn-In**
Receive equity immediately upon closing the deal in exchange for your role in sourcing, structuring, or executing the acquisition. The seller grants you equity for the value you bring (deal expertise, network, time invested).

**When to use:** You are bringing deal expertise or a buyer to the table and negotiate equity as compensation instead of a cash fee.

---

### **Event Milestone Earn-In**
Equity vests when you hit specific milestones (first customer win, revenue target, product launch, key hire). Aligns your incentives with business growth.

**When to use:** The seller wants you to prove value before granting full equity, or you want to tie your ownership to performance.

---

## Family B: Seller-Financed / Owner-Carry

### **Owner Carry (unsecured, no-interest, balloon)**
The seller finances part or all of the purchase price with an unsecured promissory note, no interest, with a balloon payment at the end of the term (e.g. 5 years). You make small or zero monthly payments and pay the balance when you refinance or sell.

**When to use:** The seller is motivated by certainty of close and tax benefits (installment sale spreads tax liability). Common in owner-operator exits where the seller trusts the buyer.

---

### **Owner Carry (secured, with interest, straight amortization)**
The seller finances with a secured promissory note (collateralized by business assets), charges interest (market rate or below), and you make regular monthly payments over 5-7 years.

**When to use:** The seller wants security (lien on assets) and income stream. You get leverage without a bank. This is the most common seller-financing structure.

---

### **Earnout**
Part of the purchase price is contingent on future performance (revenue retention, profit growth, customer retention). If the business hits targets, the seller receives the earnout. If not, you pay less.

**When to use:** You are uncertain about revenue stability or want to shift risk to the seller. Seller is confident in the business and willing to bet on future results.

---

## Family C: Seller Consulting / IP Offsets

### **Seller Consulting (performance-based)**
The seller stays on as a consultant post-close, and their consulting fee is applied to the purchase price. Fee is tied to performance (revenue retained, customers transitioned, systems documented).

**When to use:** You need the seller's knowledge to transition the business, and the seller is willing to stay involved. Reduces cash at closing.

---

### **Seller IP Royalty**
The seller retains ownership of intellectual property (brand, software, process, content) and licenses it to you for a royalty (% of revenue). The royalty payments reduce the purchase price over time.

**When to use:** The business depends on IP the seller created, and the seller is willing to license it instead of selling outright. Common in content businesses, software, and branded products.

---

## Family D: Asset Carveouts & Consignment

### **Asset Carveouts**
Remove non-essential assets from the deal (real estate, excess inventory, equipment you do not need) and negotiate a lower purchase price. The seller keeps or sells the carved-out assets separately.

**When to use:** The asking price includes assets you do not want. Carving them out reduces the price and simplifies the deal.

---

### **Seller Inventory Consignment**
The seller keeps ownership of inventory and you pay for it as it sells (consignment). Reduces upfront cash needed and shifts inventory risk to the seller.

**When to use:** The business has significant inventory (retail, distribution, manufacturing) and you want to avoid tying up cash in unsold stock.

---

## Family E: Stock Buy-Back & Merger

### **Sell & Stock Back + Put-Call**
You acquire 100% of the business, then immediately sell a portion (e.g. 20-40%) back to the seller with a put-call option. The put lets the seller force you to buy their remaining stake at a future date; the call lets you buy it back. This reduces your cash at closing.

**When to use:** The seller wants partial liquidity now but is willing to stay involved and share risk. You reduce upfront cash and align incentives.

---

### **Roll-Up**
Acquire multiple businesses in the same industry and merge them into one entity. Use the combined entity's increased value (economies of scale, higher multiple) to fund future acquisitions or refinance.

**When to use:** You are building a portfolio in a fragmented industry (home services, agencies, clinics). Each acquisition increases the value of the whole.

---

## Family G: Debt Assumption & Restructuring

### **Assume Target Debt**
Take on the seller's existing debt (equipment loans, SBA loan, credit lines) as part of the purchase price. The assumed debt reduces the cash you need to pay the seller.

**When to use:** The seller has debt with favorable terms, and the lender allows transfer without triggering due-on-sale. Confirm lender consent before closing.

---

### **Assume Seller's Personal Debt**
The seller has personal debt (mortgage, credit cards, personal loans) unrelated to the business. You agree to pay off this debt as part of the deal structure, reducing the cash the seller needs at closing.

**When to use:** The seller is financially stressed and needs debt relief. You structure the deal so paying off their debt counts toward the purchase price.

---

## Family H: Lender / Institutional Financing

### **SBA 7(a) Loan**
U.S. Small Business Administration loan designed for business acquisitions. Typically requires 10-20% down, seller can finance part of the equity injection, terms are favorable (long amortization, lower rates).

**When to use:** You need institutional financing and qualify for SBA (business size, use of proceeds). Can be combined with seller financing to reduce your cash at closing.

---

### **Revenue-Based Financing (RBF)**
A lender (e.g. Lighter Capital, Clearco) provides capital in exchange for a % of monthly revenue until a repayment cap is hit. No equity dilution, no fixed monthly payment.

**When to use:** The business has predictable recurring revenue (SaaS, subscription, service contracts) and you want to avoid giving up equity or personal guaranty.

---

## Family J: Pre-Sale / Promotion Funding

### **4DCM (4 Day Cash Machine)**
Run a promotion to the target business's customer list BEFORE closing (with seller permission). Use the revenue generated to fund part or all of the down payment. The promotion is structured as a "test" or "pre-sale" and executed in 4 days.

**When to use:** The business has a large email list or customer base, and the seller is open to a pre-close promotion. This generates real cash from the business's own assets before you own it.

---

### **Rev Share Pre-Sale (50/50)**
Partner with the seller to run a joint promotion to their customer list. Split the revenue 50/50. Your half goes toward the down payment or purchase price.

**When to use:** The seller is motivated and willing to co-promote. You generate cash and test demand before closing. This shifts the tax burden (the seller pays tax on their 50%) and funds your buy.

---

## Family K: Credit / Round-Robin / Short-Term Bridges

### **Credit Card Advance**
Use business credit cards to fund part of the down payment or closing costs. Pay off the balance from the business's cash flow post-close.

**When to use:** You have access to business credit (not personal) and the business generates enough cash flow to pay off the balance quickly (30-90 days). Use cautiously — high interest if not paid fast.

---

## Family L: Supplier / Vendor / AR / Inventory Financing

### **Supplier Invests**
A key supplier (the business's largest vendor) invests capital in exchange for equity, a revenue share, or guaranteed future orders. The investment funds part of the acquisition.

**When to use:** The business is a major customer for a supplier, and the supplier wants to protect or grow the relationship. Common in manufacturing, distribution, and retail.

---

### **AR (Accounts Receivable) Financing**
Borrow against the business's outstanding invoices (AR). A lender advances 70-90% of the AR value, you collect from customers, and repay the lender. Use the advance to fund the down payment.

**When to use:** The business has significant AR (B2B services, consulting, agencies) and you need short-term cash to close. Repay from collected invoices post-close.

---

## How to use these strategies

1. **Never rely on one strategy alone.** Build a STACK of 3-5 strategies to get to $0 cash at closing.
2. **Match strategies to the deal.** If the seller is retiring and wants certainty, use seller financing + earnout + consulting offset. If the business has inventory, add consignment. If it has a customer list, test 4DCM.
3. **Show the math.** For every STACK, calculate: Strategy | Amount | Terms | Timing. Show the seller what they receive at closing vs. over time.
4. **Frame it as win-win.** Seller benefits: tax spread (installment sale), certainty of close, legacy protection, income stream, involvement (if they want it).
